How Much to Tip a Server

Your complete restaurant tipping guide

Home › Blog › How Much to Tip a Server

Published July 1, 2026 · 4 min read

Tipping a server is one of those things everyone does, but few people feel completely confident about. How much is standard? Does it change based on the restaurant type? What if the service was just okay? This guide breaks it all down clearly, so you can tip with confidence every time.

Quick Answer

The standard tip for a server in the United States is 15–20% of the pre-tax bill. For excellent service, 20–25% is widely appreciated. For poor service, 10–15% is acceptable — dropping below 10% or leaving nothing sends a strong message and should be reserved for genuinely bad experiences.

The Standard Tip Percentages at a Glance

Service Quality Tip Percentage
Exceptional (above and beyond)25% or more
Excellent20–25%
Good (standard)18–20%
Average15–18%
Below average / poor10–15%

Why Servers Depend on Tips

In most U.S. states, servers are classified as tipped employees. This means their base hourly wage — often as low as $2.13 federally, though many states have higher minimums — is far below standard minimum wage. Tips make up the bulk of their take-home pay. Restaurants also frequently use a tip pool, meaning part of your server's tips are shared with bussers, food runners, and bartenders. When you tip well, you're often helping a whole team.

What Affects How Much You Should Tip

Type of Restaurant

At a fine dining establishment, 20% is the floor — servers at upscale restaurants provide a higher level of attentiveness and often have extensive wine and menu knowledge. At a casual sit-down spot, 15–20% is standard. At a diner or family restaurant, 18–20% is still appreciated but the pressure is slightly lower.

Level of Service

Consider how attentive your server was, whether your drinks stayed full, how food issues were handled, and the overall pace of the meal. Poor service due to the kitchen — not the server — shouldn't cost your server their tip.

Group Size

Many restaurants automatically add an 18–20% gratuity for large parties (usually 6 or more). Always check your bill before tipping — you don't want to accidentally double-tip.

Special Circumstances

  • Holidays and peak hours: Servers work harder during busy shifts. Bumping up 2–3% is a kind gesture.
  • Complex orders or substitutions: More work for the server — a slightly higher tip acknowledges that.
  • Comped items: If the restaurant comps (removes) an item from your bill, tip as if it were still there.

Pre-Tax vs. Post-Tax: Does It Matter?

Etiquette guides traditionally say to tip on the pre-tax subtotal, since tax isn't part of the service. In practice, most people tip on the total — the difference on a typical bill is only a dollar or two. Either is perfectly acceptable.

The Bottom Line

When in doubt, 20% is the safe, universally accepted standard in American dining culture. It's easy to calculate (just double the tax on your bill in most states), it reflects genuine appreciation, and it makes a meaningful difference to the person who served you. Reserve going lower for situations where service was genuinely lacking — and don't let kitchen mistakes penalize the person who brought your food.

Good tipping isn't about perfection. It's about being a fair, thoughtful guest — and that's something any server will notice.
